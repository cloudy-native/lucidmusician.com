## AI Generator Tab: Initial View

- **Establish the key**, which will guide the generated sequences.
- **Set the start chord**, inversion, and variant.
- **Pick from a growing list of distinct AI harmonic models.** The foundations of modern harmonic structure and techniques were laid by Renaissance and Baroque masters. These timeless fundamentals sound as fresh today as they did centuries ago—perfect for any contemporary or traditional genre you're composing in.
- **Set the note length.**
- **The Period knob** introduces modern sequence variations. Dial it all the way right for more distant chords and surprises.
- **Specify the number of bars.** We suggest starting small with 8 chords and linking sections you like to build longer sequences.

Click *Generate*.

![Launch LucidHarmony](images/click-generate.png)

You'll see chord names in standard notation alongside the corresponding chords in the given key. Click *Generate* as many times as you like for fresh sequences.

We've divided the workflow into two distinct parts: the generator and the voicer. Once you have a sequence you love, revoice it until it fits perfectly. The default voicing offers a balanced approach you can use right away.

You can tweak voicing heuristics independently to dial in more (or less) traditional music theory. For ambient tracks, for example, wide-open chords may shine. *Centering voices* keeps each part around the middle of the range. Enable *Common Tones* to extend MIDI notes across chords where it matters. *Stepwise* and *Contrary Motion* apply classic voice-leading principles.

Drag MIDI to your DAW or simply play the track to hear it. The current chord is highlighted.

![Launch LucidHarmony](images/instant-gratification.png)

## Harmonic Explorer

Build your own sequences step-by-step with powerful AI as your guide. In the *Harmonic Explorer* tab, click chords to assemble a progression. Close (related) chords and harmonic recommendations cluster near the UI's center. Remote or experimental chords appear further out.

![Launch LucidHarmony](images/harmonic-explorer-1.png)

The size of each next-chord bubble reflects how well-established music theory deems it a fit. If you know about chords and a bit of music theory, you can see that a VII chord after iv65 is a strong choice. But if theory isn't your thing or experimental harmony is more your style, just click around—you'll stay harmonically on track.
